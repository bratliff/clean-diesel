More Information

The term Clean Diesel refers to both innovative diesel engine technology as well as the latest diesel fuel for light vehicles. In contrast to traditional diesel, Clean Diesel excels by the fact that the new generation of engines and fuel meets the strictest emission regulations in the U.S. (issued by the state of California) while increasing mileage: Clean Diesel fuel contains less than 15 parts per million of sulphur; Clean Diesel vehicles manage more miles per gallon, while reducing CO2 emissions up to 30 percent, compared to corresponding gas models. Since Clean Diesel is not only cleaner but also more efficient, the new Clean Diesel vehicles are friendlier to both the environment and consumers wallets throughout the U.S. The original diesel technology was invented by Rudolf Diesel more than 125 years ago. See here for more information http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rudolf_Diesel.

A clean diesel light vehicle is 25% more economical than a corresponding gasoline model. This means that even if diesel fuel costs slightly more than gasoline in the US, clean diesel light vehicles manage more miles per gallon, which more than compensates for this.

No other powertrain has a greater operating range:on a full tank a German medium segment car cantravel around 800 miles on the highway and a CUV or premium car covers approximately 550 to 700 miles.

Clean Diesel engines demonstrate up to 25 % higher efficiency in comparison with modern gas engines, thus facilitating significantly larger ranges. This implies that the diesel engine makes a higher contribution to the achievement of fuel economy objectives, which in turn leads to significantly fewer trips by the customer to the gas station.

Trips to the tank calculations.

For Diesel, the multiplier is 0.46 x Number Of Miles Traveled Per Day = Annual Trips Per Year

For Gas, the multiplier is 0.71 x Number Of Miles Traveled Per Day = Annual Trips Per Year

Diesel


One tank of clean diesel is based on the Volkswagen Passat TDI. The Passat TDI holds 18.5 gallons per tank, gets 43 mpg on highways and therefore gets 795 miles per tank.3 ("z" miles per day X 365 days per year) / 795 gallons per tank (or trip) = 0.46z

Gas


One tank of gas is based on the Toyota Camry, which was the second-most-sold-car in 2011 that was not a pickup truck.1 The Camry holds 17 gallons per tank, gets 28 mpg on highways and therefore gets 476 miles per tank.2 In order to have a fair comparison, the Diesel and Gas vehicle must hold the same gallons per tank, so for calculating purposes 18.5 gallons per tank will be used.

With this, a Camry would get 518 miles per tank.
("n" miles per day X 365 days per year) / 518 gallons per tank (or trip) = 0.71n

In the summer of 2011, U.S. Secretary of Transportation, Ray LaHood, was already emphasizing that the U.S. could save 1.4 million barrels of oil every day if one-third of all cars were fitted with Clean Diesel power trains1.

Clean Diesels have such low (classical) pollutant emissions that they meet the strict limit values in all 50 federal states, even in California (AdBlue and SCR).

Modern diesel power trains have developed to reach the status of high-performance, fuel-efficient, sophisticated and, above all, clean engines. As a result of the emissions legislation, exhaust emissions from diesel engines have been reduced by up to 98 percent since the start of the nineties.2

CO2 Calculations

The Empire State building has 37 million cubic feet of volume.3 There are 8.741 square cubic feet in one pound of CO2.4 Therefore, the Empire State building can hold 4,232,925 pounds of CO2. If 1/3 of Americans switched to Diesel vehicles, 1.4M barrels of oil would be saved. From each barrel, 19 gallons of motor gas is created5. From each gallon of gas, 20 pounds of CO2 are emitted6. That means saving 1.4M barrels of oil is equivalent to 532M pounds of CO2 that would not be emitted from motor gas. This means that the amount of CO2 saved would be able to fill 125 Empire State buildings every day.
